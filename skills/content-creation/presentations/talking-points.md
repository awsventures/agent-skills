## Talking Points Specifications

### Structure Requirements
- **Format**: Bullet-style notes (not full scripts)
- **Length**: Varies by presentation type (see below)
- **Organization**:
  - Hook or opening
  - Key messages (3-5 main points)
  - Supporting details for each point
  - Transitions between sections
  - Closing and call-to-action
- **Tone**: Conversational but professional

### Talking Points Types

**Presentation Speaker Notes** (per slide):
- Key message for this slide (one sentence)
- Supporting details (2-4 bullets)
- Transition to next slide
- Anticipated questions (if applicable)
- Timing note (e.g., "spend 2 minutes here")

**Meeting Talking Points** (1-2 pages):
- Meeting objective (what you want to achieve)
- Opening statement (how to start)
- Key points to make (numbered, prioritized)
- Supporting data or examples
- Potential objections and responses
- Desired outcome and next steps

**Interview Talking Points** (2-3 pages):
- Your key narrative (30-second pitch)
- Main accomplishments (3-5 specific examples)
- Answer frameworks for common questions
- Questions to ask interviewer
- Closing statement

**Media/PR Talking Points** (1 page):
- Core message (one key takeaway)
- Three supporting points (brief, quotable)
- Bridging phrases (to redirect questions)
- What NOT to say (topics to avoid)
- Key statistics or facts

### Content Elements
- **Concise**: Bullets, not paragraphs
- **Memorable**: Use specific examples and numbers
- **Flexible**: Framework, not script (allows natural delivery)
- **Prioritized**: Most important points first
- **Actionable**: Clear on what you're asking for

## Prompt for Talking Points Creation

Before creating talking points, gather this information:

1. **Context**: What's the speaking situation?
   - Presentation
   - Meeting
   - Interview
   - Media/public speaking
   - Other

2. **Audience**:
   - Who will you be speaking to?
   - What's their level and background?
   - What do they care about?

3. **Objective**: What do you want to accomplish?

4. **Time Allotted**: How long will you speak?

5. **Key Messages**: What are the 3-5 main points you need to convey?

6. **Supporting Information**: What data, examples, or stories will you use?

7. **Anticipated Challenges**:
   - Difficult questions expected?
   - Objections or concerns to address?

8. **Desired Outcome**: What should happen after you speak?

9. **Format Preference**:
   - Brief bullets (quick reference)
   - Detailed notes (comprehensive guide)

## Example Outputs

### Example: Presentation Talking Points (for Automated Testing Proposal)

**Presentation**: Automated Testing Framework Proposal (25-minute presentation)

**Audience**: Engineering leadership (CTO, VP Engineering, 3 directors)

**Objective**: Get approval for $5K proof-of-concept, with path to $75K full implementation

---

**OPENING** (1 minute)

**Slide 1: Title Slide**

Start with the problem, not the solution:
• "We lost two major deals in Q4 because we couldn't deliver features fast enough"
• "Our release cycle has tripled from 2 weeks to 6 weeks"
• "Today I'm proposing a solution that gets us back to 2-week releases while improving quality"

**Timing**: Keep this under 1 minute, jump straight to problem

---

**SECTION 1: THE PROBLEM** (4 minutes)

**Slide 2: Current Testing Process Can't Keep Pace**

Key message: Manual testing is the bottleneck
• We're testing the same way we did 5 years ago (manual, slow)
• But our codebase is 3x larger and changes 2x faster
• Numbers tell the story: 120 hours per release, only 60% coverage

Transition: "Let's look at what this is costing us..."

**Slide 3: Business Impact**

Hit the money and competitive angle:
• $15K per month just fixing production defects (that's $180K/year)
• Mention the two lost deals specifically (if you can share names/value)
• Developer frustration is real - check the latest survey scores

**Pause for questions**: "Before I show the solution, any questions on the problem?"

---

**SECTION 2: THE SOLUTION** (6 minutes)

**Slide 4: Proposed Solution**

Keep this high-level:
• "Automated testing framework" - explain briefly (tests run automatically on every code change)
• Use the test pyramid visual to show the layers
• Key point: This catches issues in minutes, not weeks

**Slide 5: How It Works**

Walk through the flow:
• Developer commits code → automated tests run immediately
• If tests pass, code moves forward; if fail, developer gets instant feedback
• No more waiting days for manual testing
• Emphasize: "Developers get feedback in 10 minutes instead of 3 days"

**Slide 6: Expected Outcomes**

This is your money slide - spend time here:
• Point to the table: 85% defect detection (up from 60%)
• Testing time: 120 hours → 20 hours (that's 100 hours saved per release)
• Release cycle: back to 2 weeks
• Production incidents: down 70%

**Anticipate question**: "How confident are you in these numbers?"
• Response: "These are industry benchmarks from companies our size. I'm being conservative - some see even better results."

---

**SECTION 3: INVESTMENT AND TIMELINE** (5 minutes)

**Slide 7: ROI**

Lead with payback period:
• "$75K investment pays back in 6 months"
• Year 1 ROI is 60%
• After year 1, it's mostly savings (just $10K annual renewal)

**Slide 8: Implementation Timeline**

Walk through phases:
• 12 weeks total, broken into three phases
• Point out: "We're not trying to do everything at once"
• Phase 1 is foundation (tools, training)
• Phases 2-3 build coverage incrementally

**Slide 9: Budget Breakdown**

Quickly review the $75K:
• Largest chunk is tools ($35K) - these are enterprise-grade, not free tier
• Training is $25K - includes 2-day workshop and consulting support
• Infrastructure $15K - cloud resources for running tests

Don't linger here unless there are questions.

---

**SECTION 4: RISK MITIGATION** (3 minutes)

**Slide 10: Risk Mitigation**

Address concerns proactively:
• "I know three risks you're probably thinking about..."
• Walk through table: legacy code compatibility, adoption, timeline
• Emphasize proof-of-concept approach (we're testing before committing)

**Key message**: "We're not asking for $75K blind - we're asking for $5K to prove this works"

---

**SECTION 5: THE ASK** (4 minutes)

**Slide 11: Success Metrics**

Show how we'll measure:
• These aren't vague - they're specific and measurable
• We'll track these monthly and report back

**Slide 12: The Ask**

This is your close - be direct:
• "I'm requesting approval for a 2-week proof-of-concept"
• "$5K investment, starts January 22"
• "On February 5, we'll decide together: proceed with full rollout or stop"
• "If we proceed, the full $75K budget kicks in"

**Emphasize**: "This is a low-risk decision point approach, not a $75K blind commitment"

**Slide 13: Next Steps**

Walk through timeline:
• Approval this week → POC starts Jan 22 → decision Feb 5
• If approved, full implementation Feb 15 → complete April 30
• "That puts us in position to hit 2-week release cycles by May"

---

**CLOSING** (2 minutes)

**Summary**:
• "Quick recap: We have a testing bottleneck costing us deals and $180K/year"
• "Automated testing framework solves this - proven ROI, 6-month payback"
• "Low-risk ask: $5K proof-of-concept, decision point in 2 weeks"

**Call to action**:
• "I'm looking for approval to proceed with the POC starting January 22"
• "Questions?"

---

**Q&A PREPARATION**

**Anticipated Questions and Responses**:

**Q**: "Why can't we just hire more testers?"
• **A**: "We could, but that doesn't solve the speed problem. Manual testing will always be slower than automated. Plus, hiring 2-3 QA engineers costs $300K/year vs. $75K one-time investment."

**Q**: "What if developers don't write tests?"
• **A**: "We're addressing this three ways: training, making it part of code review standards, and providing consulting support. The POC will test adoption too."

**Q**: "Can't we use free open-source tools instead of $35K commercial tools?"
• **A**: "We evaluated open-source options. For enterprise scale and support, commercial tools save time and reduce risk. But we can revisit in the POC if you want."

**Q**: "Why 12 weeks? Can we go faster?"
• **A**: "We could compress to 8-10 weeks, but that increases risk of poor adoption. 12 weeks allows proper training and incremental rollout. Happy to explore faster timeline if it's a priority."

**Q**: "What happens if the POC fails?"
• **A**: "We stop. We've spent $5K to learn it won't work for us. That's the whole point of the POC - validate before big commitment."

**Q**: "How does this compare to what [competitor] is doing?"
• **A**: "Industry standard for companies our size. [Competitor] ships weekly because they have automated testing. We're playing catch-up."

---

**TIMING BREAKDOWN**:
- Opening: 1 minute
- Section 1 (Problem): 4 minutes
- Section 2 (Solution): 6 minutes
- Section 3 (Investment): 5 minutes
- Section 4 (Risk): 3 minutes
- Section 5 (The Ask): 4 minutes
- Closing: 2 minutes
- **Total**: 25 minutes
- **Buffer**: 5 minutes for questions during presentation
- **Formal Q&A**: 10-15 minutes after

**DELIVERY NOTES**:
- Don't read slides - use them as visual anchors
- Make eye contact, especially when stating the ask
- Pause after Slide 6 (outcomes) - let the numbers sink in
- Be confident but not overselling ("This will save us" not "This might help")
- If running long, skip Slide 9 (budget breakdown) - less critical
- End on time - respect their schedule

**Why it works**:
- Opening hooks with business impact, not technical details
- Problem section establishes urgency before pitching solution
- Solution explained simply (no jargon)
- Numbers are specific and credible (not exaggerated)
- Risks addressed proactively (shows you've thought it through)
- Ask is clear and low-risk (POC, not full commitment)
- Q&A prep covers likely objections
- Timing breakdown keeps you on track

### Example: Meeting Talking Points (Budget Discussion)

**Meeting**: Q1 Budget Review with CFO

**Attendees**: CFO, VP Finance, You (Director, Enterprise Applications)

**Time**: 30 minutes

**Objective**: Secure $150K budget increase for infrastructure improvements

---

**OPENING** (2 minutes)

**How to start**:
• Thank them for the time
• "I'm here to discuss Q1 infrastructure budget - specifically requesting $150K increase"
• "I'll cover why we need this, what we'll do with it, and the cost of not funding it"

**Set expectations**: "I've got 10 minutes of content, then want your input"

---

**KEY POINT 1: CURRENT SITUATION** (3 minutes)

**Main message**: We're operating on infrastructure from 2019, and it's showing

**Supporting details**:
• Infrastructure last refreshed: 2019 (5 years old)
• User base: 2x larger today (10K → 20K users)
• Application load: 3x higher (more features, more data)
• Current capacity: 85% CPU, 90% memory (normal is 60-70%)

**Key stat**: "We're one traffic spike away from an outage"

**Example**: "Remember the Q3 slowdown during enrollment? That was hitting capacity limits."

---

**KEY POINT 2: WHAT WE NEED** (4 minutes)

**Main message**: $150K for infrastructure upgrades across three areas

**Breakdown**:
1. **Server capacity** ($80K): Add 4 application servers
   - Gets us back to healthy 65% utilization
   - Handles projected 15% user growth in 2026

2. **Database scaling** ($50K): Upgrade database tier
   - Current: running at 90% IOPS limit
   - Upgrade: 2x capacity headroom

3. **Monitoring/alerting** ($20K): Enhanced observability
   - Catch issues before they become outages
   - Currently flying blind on several systems

**What this doesn't include**: Nice-to-haves like dev environment improvements (we can defer those)

---

**KEY POINT 3: COST OF NOT FUNDING** (4 minutes)

**Main message**: Not funding this costs more than $150K

**Risk scenarios**:

**Scenario 1: Outage during peak period**
• Probability: High (we've had 2 near-misses in Q4)
• Cost: $50K-$100K per hour of downtime (revenue + reputation)
• One 4-hour outage exceeds the $150K investment

**Scenario 2: Gradual degradation**
• Users experience slow performance
• Support tickets increase (already up 20% in Q4)
• Workarounds and patches cost engineering time

**Scenario 3: Emergency spending**
• If we have an outage, we'll spend on emergency fixes
• Emergency pricing: typically 2-3x normal rates
• Plus lost productivity during crisis response

**Bottom line**: "$150K proactive investment vs. $300K+ reactive crisis spending"

---

**KEY POINT 4: RETURN AND TIMELINE** (2 minutes)

**ROI framework**:
• This isn't revenue-generating, it's risk mitigation
• Value: Avoiding $50K-$100K/hour outage cost
• Also: Maintaining performance for user growth

**Timeline**:
• Approval now → procurement in Feb → installation in March
• Fully operational by April 1
• Delay past Q1 means operating at risk through enrollment period (Q2)

**Urgency**: "Q2 is our highest-traffic period. We need this in place before then."

---

**TRANSITION TO DISCUSSION** (1 minute)

"That's my case: $150K to upgrade infrastructure that's at capacity, avoid outage risk, and support growth."

"What questions do you have? What concerns should I address?"

---

**ANTICIPATED OBJECTIONS AND RESPONSES**

**Objection**: "Can we defer this to Q2 or Q3?"
• **Response**: "We could, but Q2 is enrollment - our highest risk period. Deferring means operating at high outage risk during peak season. If we have an outage in Q2, we'll spend more fixing it than this costs proactively."

**Objection**: "Can we do this in phases? $150K is a lot."
• **Response**: "Yes, we could phase it. Priority 1 is server capacity ($80K) - that's our biggest risk. Database scaling ($50K) could defer to Q2. Monitoring ($20K) could defer to Q3. Phasing means we're still at risk for longer, but it's an option."

**Objection**: "How do we know this will actually solve the problem?"
• **Response**: "We've modeled this based on current utilization and growth projections. $150K gets us back to industry-standard 65% utilization with headroom for 15% growth. We'll track utilization monthly and report back."

**Objection**: "Why didn't we plan for this earlier?"
• **Response**: "Fair question. Growth outpaced our projections - we expected 10% user growth but saw 20%. We should have flagged this in Q3 when we first hit 80%. Lesson learned: we're implementing better capacity monitoring going forward."

**Objection**: "What if we just optimize the code instead of adding infrastructure?"
• **Response**: "We've done some optimization - improved query performance, reduced memory usage. That bought us 6 months. But we're fundamentally capacity-constrained now. Code optimization won't solve underlying hardware limits."

---

**DESIRED OUTCOME**

**Best case**: Full $150K approved for Q1
**Acceptable**: $80K approved now (server capacity), revisit database/monitoring in 30 days
**Minimum**: Agreement to reconvene with detailed technical review

**Next steps** (if approved):
1. Finalize vendor selection (1 week)
2. Purchase order processed (1 week)
3. Installation scheduled (March)
4. Report back on utilization improvements (April)

---

**CLOSING** (1 minute)

**If approved**:
• "Thank you. I'll have procurement docs ready by end of week."
• "I'll send monthly utilization reports so you can track the impact."

**If not approved**:
• "I understand budget constraints. Can we reconvene in 2 weeks with more detailed technical analysis?"
• "In the meantime, I'll document the capacity risks and our mitigation plan."

**Either way**: "I appreciate your time and consideration."

---

**MEETING STRATEGY NOTES**

**Before the meeting**:
• Send 1-page summary with key numbers (don't surprise them)
• Confirm CFO has seen infrastructure capacity reports
• Align with VP Finance on approach (they should support this)

**During the meeting**:
• Lead with business risk, not technical details
• Use their language (cost avoidance, risk mitigation, ROI)
• Be ready to phase the investment if needed
• Don't get defensive about "why didn't we plan earlier" - acknowledge and move forward

**After the meeting**:
• Send follow-up email summarizing decision and next steps
• If approved: move fast on procurement (show urgency was real)
• If not approved: document risks clearly (CYA if outage occurs)

**Why it works**:
- Opens with clear objective (no mystery about what you want)
- Problem established with specific data (not vague concerns)
- Request broken down (they can approve in phases if needed)
- Cost of inaction clearly stated ($150K vs. $300K+ crisis)
- Anticipated objections prepared (you're ready for pushback)
- Multiple outcomes defined (best, acceptable, minimum)
- Respectful of their constraints (budget pressures are real)
